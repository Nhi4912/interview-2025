### Detailed LP Breakdown

#### Customer Obsession
```
DEFINITION:
"Leaders start with the customer and work backwards. They work
vigorously to earn and keep customer trust."

WHAT THEY'RE LOOKING FOR:
• Customer-first thinking
• Understanding user needs
• Measuring customer impact
• Going above and beyond for users

SAMPLE QUESTIONS:
• "Tell me about a time you put the customer first"
• "Describe a situation where you had to balance customer needs
   with business constraints"

STORY ELEMENTS NEEDED:
• Identified customer pain point
• Made decision prioritizing customer
• Measured customer impact
• Advocated for customer internally

FRONTEND EXAMPLE:
"I noticed our checkout flow had high abandonment. Rather than
just optimizing the existing flow, I conducted user interviews
to understand frustrations. I discovered users wanted to see
total cost earlier. I advocated for this change despite
resistance from the business team who wanted to show it later.
After implementing early cost display, abandonment dropped 20%."
```

#### Ownership
```
DEFINITION:
"Leaders are owners. They think long term and don't sacrifice
long-term value for short-term results. They act on behalf of
the entire company, beyond just their own team."

WHAT THEY'RE LOOKING FOR:
• Taking responsibility beyond your scope
• Long-term thinking
• Not saying "that's not my job"
• Proactive problem identification

SAMPLE QUESTIONS:
• "Tell me about a time you went beyond your job description"
• "Describe a time you took ownership of something that wasn't
   your responsibility"

STORY ELEMENTS NEEDED:
• Identified problem outside your scope
• Took initiative without being asked
• Thought about long-term impact
• Didn't blame others

FRONTEND EXAMPLE:
"I noticed our API error handling was inconsistent across teams,
causing poor user experiences. Though it wasn't my responsibility,
I created a centralized error handling library with retry logic
and user-friendly messages. I documented it and presented to
other teams. It's now used across 5 teams and reduced support
tickets by 30%."
```

#### Dive Deep
```
DEFINITION:
"Leaders operate at all levels, stay connected to the details,
audit frequently, and are skeptical when metrics and anecdote
differ."

WHAT THEY'RE LOOKING FOR:
• Root cause analysis
• Not accepting surface-level answers
• Understanding technical details
• Data-driven decisions

SAMPLE QUESTIONS:
• "Tell me about a time you had to dig deep to solve a problem"
• "Describe a time when data and intuition disagreed"

STORY ELEMENTS NEEDED:
• Investigated beyond obvious symptoms
• Found root cause
• Used data to validate
• Questioned assumptions

FRONTEND EXAMPLE:
"Our lighthouse score suddenly dropped. Initial analysis blamed
a recent feature. I dug deeper - profiled the app, analyzed
bundle sizes, checked network requests. Found the real cause:
a third-party analytics script had silently updated and now
loaded 500KB of unused code. Fixed it by switching to their
lite version. Score improved 25 points."
```

#### Deliver Results
```
DEFINITION:
"Leaders focus on the key inputs for their business and deliver
them with the right quality and in a timely fashion."

WHAT THEY'RE LOOKING FOR:
• Track record of delivering
• Overcoming obstacles
• Prioritization
• Meeting commitments

SAMPLE QUESTIONS:
• "Tell me about your most significant professional achievement"
• "Describe a time you delivered something under a tight deadline"

STORY ELEMENTS NEEDED:
• Clear deliverables
• Overcame challenges
• Met or exceeded expectations
• Quantified results

FRONTEND EXAMPLE:
"Our startup needed to launch a new product in 8 weeks for a
conference. I broke down the work, identified critical path,
and cut scope to MVP features. Worked with design to simplify
complex flows. Implemented core features in 6 weeks, leaving
2 weeks for testing. Launched successfully, acquired 500 users
at the conference, and raised our seed round."
```

#### Earn Trust
```
DEFINITION:
"Leaders listen attentively, speak candidly, and treat others
respectfully. They are vocally self-critical, even when doing
so is awkward."

WHAT THEY'RE LOOKING FOR:
• Building relationships
• Honest communication
• Admitting mistakes
• Giving/receiving feedback

SAMPLE QUESTIONS:
• "Tell me about a time you received difficult feedback"
• "Describe a time you had to deliver bad news"

STORY ELEMENTS NEEDED:
• Honest communication
• Vulnerability
• Building or repairing trust
• Self-awareness

FRONTEND EXAMPLE:
"In code review, I strongly criticized a colleague's approach.
Later, I realized my feedback was harsh and demotivating. I
apologized directly, acknowledged my communication was poor,
and asked how they preferred to receive feedback. We established
a better process - questions before criticism. Our collaboration
improved significantly after that."
```

```

│                    GOOGLEYNESS TRAITS                           │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
```
